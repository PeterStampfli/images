Kaleidoscope

A triangle of two straight lines and a circle arc make a kaleidoscope. How to find rapidly the reflections that map a point into this triangle?

The straight lines meeting at an angle of 180o/k generate a dihedral group with k-fold rotational symmetry.
This group generates images of the circle arc of the triangle. They make a polygon around the center of the image.
Inside the polygon the dihedral symmetry maps any point into the triangle.

Map points outside the polygon with inversions at the circles.
Use "ray tracing" to choose the circle: Draw a straight line between point of observation and current mapped pixel. Use the circle that intersects first with this line.
This is easy for a point of observation at the center and dihedral symmetry.
Repeat until the point is inside the polygon.

For a sum of angles > 180o we get a Poincaré disc with a periodic decoration.
For a sum of angles = 180o we get the usual Euclidean kaleidoscopes.
For a sum of angles < than 180o we get a stereographic projection of a repeating decoration of the sphere.

Zoom in at the border of the Poincaré disc to see the repeating pattern and get a transition to a Poincaré plane.

The distance between points and the boundary of the Poincaré disc increases roughly expenentially with the number of circle inversions.
But this increases the mapped size of output image pixels. If this size is much larger than the input image pixels we can get aliasing and noise.
